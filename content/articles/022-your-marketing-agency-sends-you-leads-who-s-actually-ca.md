## Article 46: Your Marketing Agency Sends You Leads. Who's Actually Calling Them?

You're paying your marketing agency $5,000, $10,000, maybe $20,000 a month. They're running Google Ads, managing your LSAs, doing SEO, maybe some social media. And they send you reports that look great: 150 leads this month! Cost per lead is down! Impressions are up!

But here's the question nobody's asking: who's calling those 150 leads?

### The Broken Handoff

Marketing agencies generate leads. That's their job. They put your firm in front of people searching for legal help, get them to click, and capture their information.

But the agency's job ends when the lead hits your CRM. From there, it's on you.

And "on you" usually means:
- Your intake coordinator sees the lead notification (eventually)
- They call when they get around to it (20 minutes to 4 hours later)
- If the lead doesn't answer, they leave a voicemail
- Maybe they follow up once or twice
- The lead either answers or it doesn't
- On to the next one

Your agency delivered the lead. You fumbled the follow-up. The lead signed with another firm. And when you look at the numbers, you blame the agency for "bad leads."

Sound familiar?

### The Data Your Agency Doesn't Show You

Your marketing agency shows you lead volume, cost per lead, and maybe click-through rates. What they don't show you — because they can't — is:

- How long it took your team to respond to each lead
- How many leads were never contacted at all
- How many leads were contacted once and then abandoned
- How many leads went to voicemail and never got a callback

If your agency could show you that data, the conversation would change very quickly. Because the problem isn't lead quality. The problem is lead handling.

### The Speed Gap Is Costing You Fortune

Here's a scenario that plays out every single day:

Your agency generates a lead at 2:47pm. The prospect just searched "personal injury lawyer near me," clicked your ad, and filled out your form. They're motivated. They're ready.

Your intake coordinator is on another call. She sees the notification at 3:15pm — 28 minutes later. She calls. Voicemail. She leaves a message and moves on.

The prospect, who filled out four contact forms at 2:47pm, answered the first firm that called at 2:49pm. They had a great conversation. They booked a consultation. By the time your team called at 3:15pm, the prospect was already committed to someone else.

Your agency did their job perfectly. Your intake process failed.
