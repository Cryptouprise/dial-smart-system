The Empty Chair Problem

April 01, 2026 6 min read By Call Boss Team

Overview

There is a particular kind of frustration that every managing partner knows. You spent $200 to generate the lead. Your intake team spent 20 minutes qualifying them. You blocked 45 minutes on an attorney's calendar. The conference room is set up. The coffee is poured. And the chair across the table is empty. The prospect did not show up. Did not call. Did not text. Just... did not come. Your attorney sits there for 10 minutes, checks their phone, sends one email to the prospect — "We missed you today, would you like to reschedule?" — and moves on. That slot on the calendar is gone forever. That lead is almost certainly gone forever too. For most law firms, this happens on 25-40% of scheduled consultations. Read that again. A quarter to nearly half of every consultation your firm books results in an empty chair and wasted attorney time.

The True Cost of No-Shows

Let us do the math for a mid-size PI firm: - 40 consultations scheduled per month - 35% no-show rate = 14 no-shows per month - Average attorney time blocked per consultation: 45 minutes - Attorney billing rate: $400/hour - Lost attorney time: 10.5 hours/month = $4,200 in opportunity cost - Average case value of consultations that would have converted: $20,000 - Estimated conversion rate of no-shows (had they shown): 40% - Lost revenue: 14 x 40% x $20,000 = $112,000 per month That is not a typo. $112,000 per month in lost revenue from people who were interested enough to schedule a consultation but did not show up. Over a year, that is $1.34 million. For a firm that books 40 consultations per month. And that does not include the marketing spend wasted on generating those leads, the staff time spent on intake and scheduling, or the demoralizing effect on your team.

Why People No-Show (It Is Not What You Think)

The natural assumption is that no-shows are tire-kickers who were never serious. That is sometimes true. But more often, the reasons are mundane and fixable:

They Forgot

This is the number one reason. The consultation was booked on Monday for Thursday. By Thursday, life happened. The kids got sick. Work got crazy. They simply forgot they had an appointment at your office at 2 PM.

They Got Nervous

Legal consultations are intimidating. People worry about cost, about being judged, about hearing bad news about their case. Between booking the appointment and the day it arrives, anxiety builds. Without any touchpoint from your firm to reassure them, they talk themselves out of it.

They Found Another Firm

If three days passed between booking and the appointment with zero communication from your firm, that is three days where another firm's ad, another firm's follow-up, another firm's Google review could have stolen them.

The Logistics Were Wrong

They booked a 2 PM appointment and then realized they cannot get off work. But they do not want to call to reschedule because that feels awkward. So they just... do not show up.

They Resolved It Themselves

The insurance company made an offer. Their spouse talked them out of pursuing the case. The injury turned out to be minor. These are genuine no-shows, but they are a minority — and even some of these people would benefit from a consultation if someone reminded them why they scheduled it in the first place.
